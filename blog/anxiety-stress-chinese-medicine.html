Stress, Anxiety, and Overthinking: What Chinese Medicine Understands That Western Medicine Misses

Your anxiety isn't "all in your head." Chinese medicine has known that for thousands of years — and modern science is starting to agree.

By Dr. Stacy Peck, DACM, L.Ac. June 2, 2026 12 min read

Here's something I say to patients almost every week: "Tell me about your anxiety." And then I listen — not just to the word anxiety, but to what's underneath it. Because when I ask follow-up questions, the stories couldn't be more different from one person to the next.

One patient describes a tight chest, clenched jaw, and the feeling of being a pressure cooker about to blow. Another describes a foggy brain that won't stop looping on the same worry, even though she knows it's irrational. A third says she lies awake with a racing heart, a sense of dread she can't name, and a body that won't calm down no matter how many deep breaths she takes.

In Western medicine, all three of these patients may receive the same diagnosis: generalized anxiety disorder. They may be offered the same class of medication. And that medication may work beautifully for one of them and do absolutely nothing for the other two.

This is where Chinese medicine has something genuinely important to add to the conversation. Not as a replacement for Western psychiatry — I want to be clear about that — but as a complementary lens that sees distinctions where modern medicine sometimes sees sameness. In Chinese medicine, those three patients have three different patterns involving three different organ systems. They need three different treatments. And when you match the treatment to the actual pattern, the results can be remarkably fast.

Let me walk you through what we see when we look at stress, anxiety, and overthinking through the lens of Chinese medicine — and what you can start doing about it today.

The Body-Mind Map: Why Emotions Live in Organs

One of the most distinctive ideas in Chinese medicine is that emotions aren't just brain events. They have a home address in the body. Each major organ system is associated with a specific emotional landscape, and when that organ is out of balance, its corresponding emotions tend to amplify. The reverse is equally true: when you experience an emotion chronically, it begins to affect its associated organ.

This isn't metaphor. It's a foundational principle documented in the Huangdi Neijing (the Yellow Emperor's Classic of Internal Medicine), a text compiled over two thousand years ago that remains the cornerstone of Chinese medical theory. Here's the basic map:

Organ System Associated Emotion When Out of Balance
LiverAnger, frustrationIrritability, tension, feeling "stuck"
HeartJoy (and its absence)Anxiety, restlessness, insomnia
SpleenWorry, overthinkingRumination, mental fog, fatigue
LungGrief, sadnessWithdrawal, heaviness, shallow breathing
KidneyFearDeep dread, existential anxiety, weak will

Notice that "anxiety" doesn't appear in just one row. That's the key insight. What Western medicine calls anxiety can originate from the Liver (as pent-up stress), the Heart (as restless agitation), the Spleen (as mental looping), or the Kidney (as a deep, nameless dread). The treatment depends entirely on which pattern is driving your particular experience.

When I'm working with a patient, I'm reading their pulse, looking at their tongue, and asking questions that might seem odd — "Do your symptoms get worse before your period? Do you sigh a lot? Do you crave sweets? Are your hands and feet cold?" — because all of those details point me toward which organ system is at the root of their distress.

Liver Qi Stagnation: The Stress Pattern Almost Everyone Has

If I had to pick one pattern that defines modern life, it would be Liver Qi stagnation. I see it in easily 70% of the patients who walk through my door, whether they came in for anxiety, headaches, digestive problems, or menstrual irregularities. It's the pattern of being chronically stressed, overcommitted, and emotionally bottled up — and in a post-pandemic world of constant screen time, information overload, and blurred work-life boundaries, it's practically an epidemic.

In Chinese medicine, the Liver is responsible for the smooth flow of Qi — vital energy — throughout the body. Think of it as the traffic controller of your internal highway system. When the Liver is happy, energy moves freely, emotions process and release, digestion hums along, and your mood stays relatively even. When the Liver's flow gets stuck — which is what "Qi stagnation" means — everything backs up.

How Liver Qi Stagnation Feels

Tightness in the chest or ribcage. A lump in the throat. Jaw clenching. Frequent sighing (your body's attempt to move stuck Qi). Irritability that's disproportionate to the situation. Tension headaches. Neck and shoulder tightness. For women, PMS symptoms that flare with emotional stress — breast tenderness, mood swings, cramping. Digestive symptoms that worsen under stress: bloating, acid reflux, alternating constipation and loose stools. The emotional signature is frustration — the feeling of being stuck, trapped, or unable to move forward.

Sound familiar? You're not alone. This is the pattern of the person who answers "How are you?" with "Fine" while their jaw is clenched and their shoulders are up by their ears. It's the person who comes home from work and snaps at their partner over nothing, then feels guilty about it. It's the person who lies awake at night mentally composing emails they'll never send.

The Western parallel is worth noting: chronic stress activates the sympathetic nervous system (fight-or-flight), increases cortisol, and creates exactly the kind of physical tension pattern that Chinese medicine has been describing for millennia. The Liver Qi stagnation framework isn't contradicting modern stress science — it's a remarkably accurate map of the same territory, drawn from a different vantage point.

Why This Pattern Escalates

Left unaddressed, Liver Qi stagnation doesn't just stay as tension and irritability. In Chinese medicine, stuck Qi generates Heat — think of it as friction. That Heat can rise upward (causing headaches, insomnia, red eyes, ringing ears) or invade other organ systems. Most commonly, the Liver "overacts" on the Spleen, disrupting digestion. This is why so many stressed people also have IBS-type symptoms. It's also why the anxious patient often has both emotional and digestive complaints that seem unrelated on paper but are deeply connected in practice.

Spleen Deficiency: When Your Brain Won't Stop Looping

If Liver Qi stagnation is the pattern of stress, Spleen deficiency is the pattern of overthinking. And in a culture that rewards constant mental productivity — always planning, always analyzing, always optimizing — it's almost as common as Liver stagnation.

In Chinese medicine, the Spleen does far more than its Western anatomical role. It's the organ of transformation: it transforms food into energy, but it also "transforms" thoughts. The Spleen houses what's called the Yi — the mental faculty governing thinking, concentration, and memory. A strong Spleen Qi means clear thinking, sharp focus, and the ability to process a thought and move on. A weak Spleen means the mental machinery gets bogged down. Thoughts go in circles. You worry about the same thing for the fourteenth time today. You read the same paragraph three times and don't retain it.

How Spleen Deficiency Feels

Mental fatigue — especially after eating. Brain fog. Difficulty concentrating. Worry that feels "sticky" — you know it's unproductive, but you can't stop. Poor appetite or stress-eating sweets (the Spleen craves sweet flavors when it's weak). Bloating after meals. Loose stools. Feeling heavy in the limbs. Bruising easily. A general sense of being mentally and physically weighed down. The emotional signature is rumination — the same worry playing on repeat.

Here's what fascinates me: modern neuroscience has discovered the gut-brain axis — a bidirectional communication network between the digestive system and the brain, mediated by the vagus nerve, immune signaling, and neurotransmitters. Approximately 90–95% of the body's serotonin — the neurotransmitter most associated with mood regulation — is produced not in the brain but in the gut, by specialized cells in the intestinal lining. The gut microbiome directly influences serotonin synthesis, and disruptions in gut bacteria have been linked to increased anxiety and depression.

Chinese medicine described this connection — that the digestive system and the thinking mind are inseparable — more than two thousand years before we had a name for the vagus nerve. The Spleen-worry axis in TCM is, in many ways, an ancient map of what we now call the gut-brain axis. When I treat a patient's overthinking by strengthening their Spleen — and their digestion improves alongside their mental clarity — it's not magic. It's a system that was right about something fundamental.

Recent research has gone even further, identifying what scientists now call the "gut-microbiota-spleen-brain axis," recognizing the spleen as a key immune organ involved in communication between the brain, gut, and immune system. A 2022 study published in Brain Research Bulletin documented direct neural pathways between the brain and spleen through vagus nerve signaling — precisely the kind of bidirectional organ-brain communication that Chinese medicine has described for centuries.

Heart and Kidney Patterns: The Deeper Anxieties

Not all anxiety is stress-based or thought-based. Some anxiety lives deeper — in the body, in the chest, in a place that words don't easily reach. That's where the Heart and Kidney patterns come in.

Heart Patterns: The Restless Spirit

In Chinese medicine, the Heart houses the Shen — often translated as "Spirit" or "Mind." The Shen is what gives you presence, coherence, the ability to be calm and centered. When the Heart is nourished and its Blood is adequate, the Shen is settled: you sleep soundly, your eyes have a calm brightness, your thoughts are orderly.

When Heart Blood or Heart Yin becomes deficient — from chronic stress, poor sleep, overwork, blood loss, or simply aging — the Shen loses its anchor. This produces a very specific kind of anxiety: a fluttery, restless feeling in the chest. Palpitations. Startling easily. Vivid or disturbing dreams. Insomnia where you can fall asleep but wake frequently. A sense of vulnerability, as if you've lost a protective layer between you and the world.

This is the patient who tells me, "I don't even know what I'm anxious about. I just feel anxious." There's no story attached to it, no specific worry — just a pervasive unsettledness. In Western terms, this often overlaps with what's diagnosed as panic disorder or free-floating anxiety.

Kidney Patterns: The Existential Dread

The Kidney in Chinese medicine is the root of all Yin and Yang in the body — the deepest reserves. It's associated with the emotion of fear: not everyday nervousness, but the bone-deep kind. Fear of mortality. Fear of failure at a fundamental level. A sense of running on empty with nothing left in the tank.

Kidney-based anxiety tends to be quieter and more constitutional. It often shows up alongside other signs of depletion: low back weakness, knee pain, premature graying, frequent urination, poor memory, night sweats, or a feeling of cold that doesn't warm up. This is the pattern I see most often in patients who have been burning the candle at both ends for years — the executive who hasn't taken a real vacation in a decade, the new parent running on three hours of sleep, the caregiver who has given everything to everyone else.

In clinical practice, Heart and Kidney patterns often appear together. Chinese medicine even has a name for this: "Heart and Kidney not communicating." The Heart's Fire (warmth, consciousness) should descend to warm the Kidneys, and the Kidney's Water (coolness, substance) should ascend to calm the Heart. When this vertical axis breaks down, you get both the restless anxiety of the Heart and the deep exhaustion of the Kidneys — wired and tired at the same time.

What Western Medicine Sees vs. What Chinese Medicine Sees

I want to be very clear: I am not anti-Western medicine. If a patient is in crisis, SSRIs can be lifesaving. Cognitive behavioral therapy is well-evidenced and genuinely helpful. I refer patients to psychiatrists and therapists regularly. What I'm saying is that Chinese medicine adds resolution to the picture — it sees important distinctions that a single diagnosis of "generalized anxiety disorder" can sometimes flatten.

Western Approach Chinese Medicine Approach
Diagnoses anxiety as a categoryDifferentiates 6–8 distinct patterns that all present as "anxiety"
Targets neurotransmitters (serotonin, GABA)Targets the organ system driving the imbalance
Asks "What are your symptoms?"Asks "What is the root pattern generating those symptoms?"

The most powerful approach, in my clinical experience, is both. Western medicine for stabilization and Chinese medicine for pattern resolution. They're not competing — they're looking at different layers of the same person.

What the Research Actually Shows

This is where skeptics usually lean in, so let me share what the evidence says — because it's stronger than most people realize.

Multiple systematic reviews and meta-analyses of randomized controlled trials have examined acupuncture for anxiety disorders. A meta-analysis of 20 RCTs involving 1,462 participants found that manual acupuncture significantly reduced anxiety scores compared to sham acupuncture (SMD = –1.06, 95% CI: –1.74 to –0.39, p = 0.0005), and the effect was sustained at follow-up. Another meta-analysis of 14 RCTs (968 patients with generalized anxiety disorder) found acupuncture significantly reduced Hamilton Anxiety Rating Scale scores versus sham, with a mean difference of –2.71 points.

A larger review of 41 RCTs found that acupuncture alone and acupuncture combined with medication both outperformed medication alone for anxiety reduction. The combined approach showed the strongest results — which aligns with what I see in practice. Patients who use acupuncture alongside their existing treatment tend to do better than those using either approach in isolation.

Is the evidence perfect? No. Researchers consistently note that study quality varies and more large-scale, well-designed trials are needed. But the direction of the evidence is consistent and clinically meaningful — and the side-effect profile of acupuncture is vastly more favorable than that of most anxiolytic medications.

"The question is no longer whether acupuncture works for anxiety — it's how to best integrate it with other evidence-based approaches for the individual patient."

5 Things You Can Do Right Now

You don't need to visit an acupuncturist to start working with these principles today. Here are five practical strategies grounded in Chinese medicine that you can try immediately.

1. Acupressure for Anxiety: Five Points You Can Press Yourself

Acupressure uses the same points as acupuncture, but with finger pressure instead of needles. These five points are among the most well-studied and frequently used for anxiety and stress. Press each point firmly with your thumb or fingertip for 60–90 seconds, breathing slowly and deeply. You can do these anywhere — at your desk, in bed, even in a parked car before a stressful meeting.

  • LR-3 (Tai Chong) — "Great Surge": On the top of the foot, in the depression where the first and second metatarsal bones meet — about an inch and a half up from the web between the big toe and second toe. Slide your finger up from the web space until you feel a tender hollow where the bones converge. This is the primary point for moving Liver Qi stagnation. Press it when you feel frustrated, tense, or emotionally stuck.
  • PC-6 (Nei Guan) — "Inner Gate": On the inner forearm, about two finger-widths above the wrist crease, between the two central tendons (palmaris longus and flexor carpi radialis). Place three fingers across your inner wrist — the point is just above the third finger, centered between the tendons. This point calms the Heart, settles nausea, and is one of the most powerful points for acute anxiety and panic. It's also the point behind anti-nausea wristbands.
  • HT-7 (Shen Men) — "Spirit Gate": At the wrist crease, on the pinky side, in the small depression just to the thumb-side of the tendon you can feel when you flex your wrist (flexor carpi ulnaris). This point directly calms the Shen (Spirit) and is used for insomnia, anxiety, palpitations, and emotional overwhelm. Excellent before bed.
  • GV-20 (Bai Hui) — "Hundred Meetings": At the very top of the head, on the midline, at the intersection of a line drawn up from the tips of both ears. Place your fingers on both ear tips and draw a line upward — where they meet at the crown is GV-20. This point lifts the spirit, clears the mind, and calms racing thoughts. Research has shown electroacupuncture at this point reduces anxiety-like behavior by promoting oxytocin release.
  • Yintang — "Hall of Impression": The midpoint between the eyebrows, right where the bridge of the nose meets the forehead. Also called the "third eye point." Apply gentle, steady pressure or small circular motions for 30 seconds to 2 minutes. Studies have found that acupuncture at Yintang significantly lowers cortisol levels and is effective for preoperative anxiety. This is the single most calming point on the body — I use it on nearly every patient.

2. The 4-7-8 Breath (A TCM-Compatible Technique)

Chinese medicine has long emphasized that the breath is the most accessible lever for moving Qi. The extended exhale is particularly important: in TCM terms, the exhale descends Qi and calms the Liver. In Western terms, it activates the vagus nerve and shifts the nervous system from sympathetic (fight-or-flight) to parasympathetic (rest-and-digest).

Try this: Inhale through the nose for 4 counts. Hold for 7 counts. Exhale slowly through the mouth for 8 counts. Repeat four cycles. The long exhale is what does the work — it physically signals your body that you're safe. Do this before bed, during a stressful moment, or as a daily practice.

3. Eat for Your Spleen (Stop the Worry Spiral)

If overthinking is your dominant pattern, your Spleen needs support. In Chinese food therapy, the Spleen thrives on warm, cooked, easy-to-digest foods and is damaged by cold, raw, and excessively sweet foods. This is practical, not complicated:

  • Favor: Soups, stews, congee (rice porridge), cooked root vegetables (sweet potato, squash, carrots), warm grains, ginger tea, cooked leafy greens, small amounts of lean protein
  • Reduce: Ice water, raw salads as main meals, smoothie bowls (especially in the morning), excessive dairy, sugar, and processed foods
  • Key principle: Eat breakfast. The Stomach's peak time is 7–9am and the Spleen's is 9–11am. Skipping breakfast and running on coffee is one of the fastest ways to deplete Spleen Qi — and worsen the overthinking cycle

4. Move Your Liver Qi Daily

The Liver needs movement — physical, emotional, and creative. Stagnation is its enemy. Build at least one of these into every day:

  • Physical movement: A brisk 20-minute walk, yoga (especially twists, which target the Liver area in the ribcage), dancing, swimming — anything that gets energy circulating. The key is consistent and moderate, not punishing.
  • Emotional movement: Journaling, talking to someone you trust, therapy, even crying when you need to. Unexpressed emotion is the number one cause of Liver Qi stagnation.
  • Creative movement: The Liver governs planning and vision. Creative expression — art, music, writing, gardening — gives the Liver's energy a healthy outlet.

5. Protect Your Shen at Night

The Heart-based anxiety pattern — restlessness, palpitations, disturbed sleep — is worsened by everything that overstimulates the Shen in the evening. In practical terms:

  • Screen curfew 60–90 minutes before bed (the constant stimulation agitates the Shen)
  • No news, social media, or stressful content after 8pm
  • A warm (not hot) foot soak for 15 minutes before bed — this draws energy downward from the head, calming the Heart and warming the Kidneys
  • Chamomile, jujube date (Da Zao), or longan (Long Yan Rou) tea in the evening — all gently nourish the Heart and calm the Spirit

When to Seek Treatment

Self-care is powerful, but it has limits. Consider seeing a licensed acupuncturist or Chinese medicine practitioner if:

  • Your anxiety or stress has persisted for more than a few weeks and is affecting your sleep, relationships, or ability to function
  • You're having panic attacks or physical symptoms (chest tightness, palpitations, digestive distress) that your doctor can't fully explain
  • You've tried medication and it's either not working, causing side effects, or you'd like to explore complementary options
  • You recognize yourself in more than one of the patterns described above — multiple overlapping patterns benefit from professional pattern differentiation
  • You're going through a major life transition (postpartum, perimenopause, grief, career change) and need deeper support

A good practitioner won't ask you to choose between Western and Chinese medicine. They'll work with your existing care team. The goal is always the same: get you feeling like yourself again — not a medicated version of yourself, not a white-knuckling-through-it version, but the actual, grounded, clear-headed you.

Your body has been trying to tell you what's wrong. The tight chest, the spinning thoughts, the 3am wake-ups, the knot in your stomach — those aren't just symptoms to suppress. They're information. Chinese medicine is, at its core, a system for reading that information and responding to the root, not just the branch.

And in a world that keeps handing us more reasons to feel anxious, that kind of medicine has never been more relevant.
